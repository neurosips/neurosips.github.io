Imagine you wake up in the morning feeling excited that you are going to have a cup of coffee with your friend at a new Cafe. You take a shower and put on your good clothes on. While brushing your teeth, you look in the miror and suddenly you feel confused. You desperately struggle to remember why you have put on your good clothes and where you wanted to go. You go back to your room and wonder who put a new frame on your wall. You ask your partner if she hung the new frame and she responds that you did last night while looking at you strangely. Few minutes later you ask the same question from your partner and she realizes that something is off. She calls the EMS and they bring you to the hospital.  
<br />
This is something that happened to a 70 year old woman that I had pleasure of seeing in the ED. The disease is called Transient Global Amnesia (TGA) and the characteristics are difficulty with fomring new memories (anterograde amnesia), and remembering recent events (retrograde amnesia), howerver anterograde amnesia is more severely impacted than retrograde amnesia.   
<br />
Several questions have occupied the mind of neurologists since description of TGA. Is TGA a presentation of acute stroke? Is TGA associated with a lesion in the brain and if it is, where do lesions localize to?     
<br />
In the past two decades, advanced imaging techniques have cast new light on these questions and provided some insight regarding the role of human hippocampal subregions in the formation of new memories. These studies have shown that a large number of patients with TGA have DWI restricted diffusions in the hippocampus[ref]. These lesions behave similar to ischemic lesions in other parts of the brain[ref].
