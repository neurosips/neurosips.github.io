Imagine you wake up in the morning and you are excited about having coffee with your friend in a new cafe. You take a shower and put your good clothes on. While brushing your teeth, you look in the miror and suddenly you feel confused. You desperately struggle to remember why you have your good clothes on and where you were heading to. You go back to your room and wonder who put a new frame on your wall. You ask your partner if she hung the new frame and in your amazement she says you did it yourself two nights ago. A few minutes later, you ask your partner who hung a new frame on your wall and with that question, you buy yourself a visit to the ED. 
<br />
This happened to a 70 year old woman that I had pleasure of seeing in the ED. The disease is called Transient Global Amnesia (TGA). Patients have difficulty with fomring new memories (anterograde amnesia) and remembering recent events (retrograde amnesia), howerver, anterograde amnesia is more severely impacted. The diagnosis is based on clinical presentation and defect in delayed recall test with otherwise normal cognitive functions and neurological exam.  
<br />
Several questions have occupied the mind of neurologists since description of TGA. Is TGA a presentation of acute stroke? Is TGA associated with a lesion in the brain and if it is, where do lesions localize to?     
<br />
In the past two decades, advanced imaging techniques have cast new light on these questions and provided some insight regarding the role of human hippocampal subregions in the formation of new memories. These studies have shown that a large number of patients with TGA have restricted diffusions in Diffusion-Weighted Imagig (DWI)[ref]. The lesion mostly is located in a subfield of hippocampus called CA1[ref]. Although still in controversy, some studies suggest that these lesions behave similar to ischemic lesions in other parts of the brain[ref].
